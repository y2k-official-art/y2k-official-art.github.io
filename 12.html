Movement

Movement is a hybrid bio-system that can recognize the human body movements according to the Mimosa signals. Each Mimosa in the system is equipped with a bioamplifier that transmits the plant's sensory state to the computational hub, thereby linking the system to numerous plant units.

Through artificial intelligence algorithm, the computer is able to analyze certain body movements from the plant’s perception according to its biosignals’ waveform, strength, and time of occurrence.

Collection and analysis of Mimosa signals based on human's movements

Based on the classification model developed in Apercevoir, we developed a system to predict the human's movement based on four models’ predic- tion states and time differences with the biosignals of the four Mimosas. We build a circular space and placed the four Mimosas in it. The four classification models are combined to achieve simultaneous processing of multiple signals.

The dancers are then asked to perform specific movements repeatedly in the circle, and the four sets of model-predicted states are continuously collected during the action period, and finally the four sets of predicted state sequences are concatenated and labeled with specific movement. This dataset was then used to train a classification model that was able to predict the specific movements of human.

To allow the audience to understand the perceptions of the plants, when the audience makes a movement sound the plants, the perceptual messages from the plants are decoded and used as parameters to generate a feedback sound field. At the same time, the energy of the sound vibrates the water inside the light installation, creating an instantaneous change in the water wave pattern. The audience can directly see the fluctuations of the biosignals that the plants communicate with each other.

Specific water wave pattern

When the audience walks into the system and moves his body, the system senses the movement through multiple plants and generates a specific sound field according to the specific movements. In this system, the interaction between the plants perceived by humans and the "dance" perceived by plants will be completely presented.
